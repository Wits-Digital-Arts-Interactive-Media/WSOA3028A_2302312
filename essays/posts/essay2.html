Essay 2: Digital Colonialism

Colonialism is the practice of acquiring full or partial control over another country and exploiting it for its resources. The most famous example of this is Europe’s colonisation of the Global South where Europeans would gain native people’s trust by bringing them gifts and new technologies all while extracting resources and later, capturing the people as slaves. Similarly, big tech companies today offer seemingly free products and services while extracting user data and influencing user ideologies for their own gain. This practice is known as digital colonialism[1].

Google offers various free services to users who create an account with them. This includes amongst others, 15GB of free cloud storage and an email address under their domain. Creating a Google account is necessary in order to use an Android smartphone. According to GeoPoll, 95% of South Africans have a smartphone [2] and 83.7% of those smartphones are Android devices[3]. Apple has similar policies and together with Google has 99.71% of the smartphone market share in the country. Herein lies the issue: almost every person relies on their smartphone daily, be it their sentimental photo library or navigating the world with GPS. These big American companies can make any number of new policies that are detrimental to users, and users will have no choice but to accept them as there are few to no alternative services that can fulfil their daily needs. Additionally, it is difficult and troublesome to switch over to an alternative. This is only one example of how big-tech companies entrap people by offering them free services.

Recently, the tech industry has been flooded with Generative Pre-trained Transformers (GPTs). Seemingly every big tech company is trying to implement a GPT wherever they can. A GPT’s effectiveness relies heavily on having large amounts of training data on which it can base its responses. As such, data and the rights thereto are in high demand. Especially human-generated content like copyrighted writing and conversational texts. This is because GPTs aim to create content such as writing or images that look as if a human could have created it. The collection and selling of user data is nothing new, companies have been doing it since the early days of the internet for commerce purposes: An advertisement is more likely to be successful if the person seeing it is especially interested in the particular topic. If collecting data for advertisement purposes was ethically questionable, then collecting data to imitate your writing style, voice and likeness is outright unethical. Technically users agree to this data extraction when accepting the terms and conditions: A document that is so unnecessarily long that even a legally trained person would struggle to make sense of it. Even if you did read it, declining the terms means declining all the benefits that come with it such as cloud storage and the ability to use most smartphones.

This data extraction shares a likeness to 17th-century colonialism, in that Europeans extracted resources without the explicit consent of the rightful owners of that resource, in the digital case, even though users give consent, most do so unknowingly. In a more literal sense, the very hardware that facilitates all these services requires precious minerals[1]. These minerals are mostly mined and extracted from countries foreign to the companies doing the extraction and profiting therefrom.

Another key aspect of colonialism is that of exerting control. Historically, this was done by claiming the foreign country as a colony and governing over its people. Big Tech companies exert influence by controlling what information people receive and what they don’t[4]. The primary source of most people’s information is via search engines or social media. Both of these are heavily biased based on who is interacting with them. This allows Big Tech companies to greatly influence a country’s politics and subsequently its policies, which ironically are also a government's best chance to fight digital colonialism. These companies effectively have a global monopoly on how information is spread and the world is just trusting them to not abuse it.

Software is often proprietary [5], meaning users cannot read, modify or share the source code. This implicitly takes the control out of the consumer's hands: they can use their devices, and the software thereon but they cannot make any changes or fully know how the device works. It is this secrecy and lack of control that empowers tech companies so greatly: Governments struggle to pass laws and regulations if they cannot understand what they are limiting. People struggle to trust their devices if they cannot opt out of data collection. Both users and governments are entrapped due to the way these devices are intertwined with the daily lives of citizens. As developers, we should advocate open-source software where possible, and strive to make software as customizable as possible when open-source is not possible. By putting as much control as possible in the hands of our users, we limit the control our products influence over them. Furthermore, we should have extensive and understandable documentation to assist lawmakers in understanding the workings of our products. Not only is this the ethical thing to do, but it also prevents products from being unnecessarily banned by uninformed lawmakers.
